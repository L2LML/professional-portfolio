# Closing the Adherence Gap: A DMAIC Approach to Healthcare Burnout

**Deck:** [`healthcare-burnout-dmaic.pptx`](./healthcare-burnout-dmaic.pptx)

A self-directed DMAIC case study proposing how to collect, measure, analyze, and act on burnout across medical students, hospital staff, and hospital administration — prepared as candidate work for a **Senior Project Manager, Office of Well-Being** role.

This is not a live client engagement (unlike the [LSSBB Portfolio](../LSSBB_Portfolio_Lewandowski.pptx)). It's an applied methodology proposal, grounded in real direct-care field experience with medically complex, non-verbal individuals, generalized to a hospital/academic medical center context.

## Core thesis

Burnout in a care team isn't uniform disengagement — it's **variable adherence to standard work**, and that variation is both a symptom of burnout and a cause of it: staff who stay consistent absorb the resentment and the workload gap left by those who don't. Sustaining consistency long-term requires tying advancement, assignments, and pay to it — not just low-cost recognition gestures.

## Structure (DMAIC)

| Phase | Content |
|---|---|
| **Define** | Problem framing, stakeholders (medical students, hospital staff, hospital administration), field evidence from direct-care work |
| **Measure** | Data collection plan (MBI/Mini-Z, adherence checklist, exit interviews, turnover, EHR logs, safety incident reports), segmented by staff rotation cadence, plus a sample-findings slide pulling real numbers from the companion dashboard |
| **Analyze** | Four-branch root-cause analysis (workload/staffing, accountability, reward, safety) plus an evidence-base slide cross-referencing three independent academic sources |
| **Improve** | Low-cost initiatives (checklist accountability, tied recognition, peer huddles) and higher-cost initiatives (tiered care-ladder advancement, 4-day work week + dedicated 5th-day coverage, staffing ratio increases) |
| **Control** | Pilot design, cost/impact model for leadership, sustainment mechanism (review cadence, follow-through, social visibility) |

## Evidence base

The Analyze phase includes a slide cross-referencing three independent sources spanning 40 years and two populations, all converging on the same finding: low Personal Accomplishment / lack of career advancement — not raw exhaustion — is the more consistent burnout driver. This directly supports the case study's Tiered Advancement / Care Ladder proposal.

- Baker, P., Chishty-Collins, J., & Gore, N. (2025). A systematic review of burnout among staff working in services for adults with intellectual and developmental disabilities. *Journal of Applied Research in Intellectual Disabilities*, 38(1), e13325. https://doi.org/10.1111/jar.13325
- Alexaki, P.-S., Antonopoulou, H., Gkintoni, E., Adamopoulos, N., & Halkiopoulos, C. (2025). Psychological dimensions of professional burnout in special education: A cross-sectional behavioral data analysis of emotional exhaustion, personal achievement, and depersonalization. *International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health*, 22(9), 1420. https://doi.org/10.3390/ijerph22091420
- Frith, G. H., & Mims, A. (1985). Burnout among special education paraprofessionals. *Teaching Exceptional Children*, 17(3), 225–227.
